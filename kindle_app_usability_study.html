The goal of the Amazon Kindle Multi device Usability study was to help Amazon provide its users with the most user-friendly and engaging experience across a variety of devices.


Along with a team of students, from the Usability Research Methods course at the University of Washington, I conducted an extensive usability evaluation on the iOS and OSX Kindle Applications to uncover how users interact with and utilize the features of the application in support of a variety of tasks and goals.  By testing the applications in-context with real users, our team was able to uncover several usability issues, and from those issues develop a set of design considerations that we believe would help Amazon better meet the needs and expectations of its customers. Project Teammates: Alec Martin, Natalee Ouzts, and Kendall Sahl.


My personal responsibilities on this project included, test and task design, acting a observer during testing sessions, and assisting in reporting.


Participant Recruitment

Our five participants were recruited using an online screener survey posted on personal Facebook pages and groups. Four females and one male were tested. None of the participants were regular Kindle users, but all had at least some experience using eReader applications.

• Task 1 Selected an ebook and change the font size of the text in the application so that it is easier to read.

• Task 2 Highlight a sentence or passage and then make an annotation about this sentence or passage in your own words.

• Task 3 Open the ebook to the index at the back of the book and find “________”. Then, navigate to the related section, page, or location where the topic is referenced and bookmark it.

• Task 4 Now navigate to a page or section where you have not made any highlights, annotations, or bookmarks and read as you normally would.

• Task 5 Open the Kindle application and find the section or page where you left off reading on the other device.

• Task 6 Find the bookmark you made after referencing an index topic.

• Task 7 Find the highlight and annotation you made on the previous device.

Test Environment and Data Collection

The sessions were conducted in the Allen Research Commons and Sieg Hall.  Each testing environment was set up in the same manner to the greatest extent possible, in order to ensure that participants had similar testing experiences. Both qualitative and quantitative data were collected during each session using a combination of note-taking , audio and video recordings, screen capture, and post task and post test questionnaires filled out by the participants directly. 

Data Analysis

To analyze our data, we performed two separate affinity diagramming exercises and grouped our data into logical categories and themes. By critically examining these recurring themes and referencing our quantitative data, we were able to assess the severity and specificity of each usability issue and develop informed implications and recommendations for future designs.

• Page Navigation

   Finding: There were too many ways to navigate through pages. Users rarely chose the

   most efficient method for navigating large portions of text.

   Recommendation: Design more intuitive page navigation controls that are scalable while

   remaining visible, like the "indexed list" feature from the iOS Contacts app.

• Section Navigation (Indexing)

   Finding: Users had trouble navigating from the index to a specified page number.

   Recommendation: Include a dynamic linking feature that would take the user directly to

   their intended page after clicking directly on an individual page number or index listing.

• System Navigation

   Finding: Users had trouble finding controls to change text, navigate chapters/sections,

   and store notes, highlights, and bookmarks.

   Recommendation: Label icons with text and prioritize system features for users.

Findings and Recommendations

Here are the issues we discovered during analysis in order of severity:

Many of our participants expected features that were not included in the Kindle Reader app or they just could not locate them. We also discovered glaring inconsistencies between different eBook formats that highlight issues beyond the scope of our study. In conclusion, we recommended that further usability studies be conducted with multiple eBook formats and that clear benchmarks for usability and acceptance criteria be established.

• Highlighting and Annotation

   Finding: Users had trouble finding highlighted sections and annotations that they had

   just made.

  

















   Recommendation: Implement a visually responsive state change for the notebook icon

   button in the side toolbar on the left to demonstrate its relationship to stored highlights

   and notes.

• Search

   Finding: Participants were inclined to use a search bar to find the specified page number,

   but the only search bar in the app is used to search specifically for keywords in the text.

   Recommendation: Add a search feature that accesses page numbers in addition to

   keywords.
